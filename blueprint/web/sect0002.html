2 Vector Spaces

This world introduces the fundamental theory of vector spaces over fields, establishing key properties through 6 levels of increasingly sophisticated proofs.

2.1 Vector Space Definition and Structure

Definition 11 Vector Space
✓
#

Lean declarations

  • LinearAlgebraGame.VectorSpace

A vector space \(V\) over a field \(K\) is an abelian group \((V, +)\) equipped with scalar multiplication \(\cdot : K \times V \to V\) satisfying:

  1. Distributivity over vector addition: \(\forall a \in K, \forall x,y \in V: a \cdot (x + y) = a \cdot x + a \cdot y\)

  2. Distributivity over scalar addition: \(\forall a,b \in K, \forall x \in V: (a + b) \cdot x = a \cdot x + b \cdot x\)

  3. Compatibility: \(\forall a,b \in K, \forall x \in V: (a \cdot b) \cdot x = a \cdot (b \cdot x)\)

  4. Identity: \(\forall x \in V: 1 \cdot x = x\)

2.2 Level 1: Introduction

This level provides a gentle introduction to the Vector Space World using a trivial proof to familiarize players with the game interface and the notation for scalar multiplication (\smul).

2.3 Level 2: Zero Scalar Multiplication

Theorem 12 Zero Scalar Yields Zero Vector
#

Uses

  • Definition 11

Lean declarations

  • LinearAlgebraGame.zero_smul_v

In any vector space \(V\) over field \(K\), scalar multiplication by zero yields the zero vector:

\[ \forall w \in V: (0 : K) \cdot w = (0 : V) \]
Proof ▶

We use the distributivity axiom with \(a = 0\) and observe that:

\[ 0 \cdot w = (0 + 0) \cdot w = 0 \cdot w + 0 \cdot w \]

Subtracting \(0 \cdot w\) from both sides yields \(0 = 0 \cdot w\).

2.4 Level 3: Multiplying the Zero Vector

Theorem 13 Scalar Times Zero Vector
#

Uses

  • Definition 11

Lean declarations

  • LinearAlgebraGame.smul_zero_v

For any scalar \(a \in K\), multiplication by the zero vector yields zero:

\[ \forall a \in K: a \cdot (0 : V) = (0 : V) \]
Proof ▶

Using distributivity over vector addition with \(x = y = 0\):

\[ a \cdot 0 = a \cdot (0 + 0) = a \cdot 0 + a \cdot 0 \]

Subtracting \(a \cdot 0\) from both sides gives the result.

2.5 Level 4: Scaling by Negative One

Theorem 14 Negative One Scalar
#

Uses

  • Definition 11
  • Theorem 12

Lean declarations

  • LinearAlgebraGame.neg_one_smul_v

Multiplication by \(-1\) gives the additive inverse:

\[ \forall v \in V: (-1 : K) \cdot v = -v \]
Proof ▶

We show that \((-1) \cdot v\) is the additive inverse of \(v\) by verifying:

\[ v + (-1) \cdot v = 1 \cdot v + (-1) \cdot v = (1 + (-1)) \cdot v = 0 \cdot v = 0 \]

Therefore, \((-1) \cdot v = -v\) by uniqueness of additive inverses.

2.6 Level 5: Subspaces Contain Zero

Definition 15 Subspace
✓
#

Uses

  • Definition 11

Lean declarations

  • LinearAlgebraGame.isSubspace

A subset \(W \subseteq V\) is a subspace if:

  1. \((0 : V) \in W\) (contains zero)

  2. \(\forall x, y \in W: x + y \in W\) (closed under addition)

  3. \(\forall a \in K, \forall x \in W: a \cdot x \in W\) (closed under scalar multiplication)

Theorem 16 Zero in Subspace
#

Uses

  • Definition 15

Lean declarations

  • LinearAlgebraGame.subspace_contains_zero

Every subspace contains the zero vector:

\[ \text{If } W \text{ is a subspace of } V, \text{ then } (0 : V) \in W \]
Proof ▶

This follows immediately from the first axiom in the definition of a subspace.

2.7 Level 6: Negatives in Subspaces

Theorem 17 Subspace Closed Under Negation
#

Uses

  • Definition 15
  • Theorem 14

Lean declarations

  • LinearAlgebraGame.subspace_neg

Subspaces are closed under additive inverses:

\[ \forall W \text{ subspace}, \forall x \in W: -x \in W \]
Proof ▶

Since \(W\) is closed under scalar multiplication and \(x \in W\), we have:

\[ -x = (-1) \cdot x \in W \]

using the result from Level 4 that \((-1) \cdot x = -x\).

2.8 Summary

This world establishes fundamental properties of vector spaces:

  • The behavior of scalar multiplication with zero (both zero scalar and zero vector)

  • The relationship between scalar multiplication by \(-1\) and additive inverses

  • Basic properties of subspaces, particularly their closure properties

These results form the foundation for more advanced topics in linear algebra, including linear independence, span, and linear transformations explored in subsequent worlds.
